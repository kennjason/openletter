Open Letter to Docker, Inc

 

May 1, 2017

 

Steve Singh

Solomon Hykes

Docker, Inc.

144 Townsend Street

San Francisco, CA 94107

 

Mr. Singh, Mr. Hykes:

As an active member of the Go, Docker, and Kubernetes communities, I’m at a loss for words with regards to the lack of management intervention in both past and recent incidents of harassment of colleagues in the community by Docker team members.

Our communities are the only reason that we succeed, and for that success to be viable long term, each member of the community must feel safe, valued, and comfortable.  As a corporation, Docker is responsible for the behavior of its employees in public and private spaces when they operate under the purview of Docker’s umbrella.  Your responsibility goes beyond the fiduciary responsibility to your shareholders; it extends to your customers, vendors, partners, and users.  Your success hinges on your ability to provide value to customers, and that can only be done with the help and evangelism of a happy and prosperous community.

The Go, Docker, and Kubernetes communities are not willing to accept harassment in any form.  As a member of these communities, I demand better than this of myself and those around me.  This group of people has been the most caring and welcoming group I’ve ever seen in more than 25 years of programming.  I refuse to stand by idly and allow bad actors to destroy the goodwill and harmony we’ve worked so hard to achieve.  Our community is great because we made it this way.  Each of us: your users, your contributors, your partners, and your employees have chosen to belong to this group not just because of the value of a container solution, but because of the value of the community of helpful and caring people that comes with it.

Docker has found technical and commercial success by making cutting edge technology accessible to a broad population of developers and by fostering an ecosystem of related projects that work well together.  Tolerating and therefore tacitly endorsing bullying and intimidating behavior by your own team members undermines the ingredients of your success and threatens to sour the goodwill that has made Docker a de facto standard.  The treatment you tolerate toward contributors to your ecosystem foreshadows the treatment you will tolerate toward the customers who invest in adopting your products.

I demand that you take immediate action to permanently end this harassment.   It’s not right for the people in your community, and it’s not right for Docker, Inc.  Failure to fix this breach in our trust will result in this community finding another place to call home.  Docker must hold its employees to a code of conduct for public and private behavior.

 

Looking forward to your prompt response,

 

Brian Ketelsen
